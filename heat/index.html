Why this matters — and how to read this dashboard

The problem. Heat advisories, news rankings, and much of India's public heat conversation run on dry-bulb air temperature — the number a regular thermometer reads. But the body doesn't overheat by temperature alone: it cools itself by evaporating sweat, and humidity throttles that. Radiant sun load and wind change the picture further. Two cities at 35°C can carry very different physiological risk.

What this dashboard does. It re-ranks ~50 large Indian cities twice a day-window: once by ordinary peak temperature, once by estimated WBGT — an occupational heat-stress metric that folds in humidity, sun, and wind. The gap between a city's two ranks is the misranking delta. Cities with a big positive delta (▲) are the ones the ordinary ranking understates today.

Why WBGT, if India doesn't use it officially? WBGT is the internationally standard occupational heat-stress metric (ISO 7243), used by militaries, sports bodies, and workplace-safety regulators worldwide, and Indian researchers have applied it in urban and occupational heat-stress studies. It isn't part of India's official heat-warning vocabulary yet — which is part of the point: this dashboard estimates it from public weather data to show what that vocabulary currently leaves out.

What to look for. The map's warm-colored dots and the chart's highlighted lines are today's climbers. The "biggest climbers" card explains why each one climbed (humidity, sun, wind) from the same data used to compute it. Note the direction is not fixed: on some days inland northern cities climb and humid coastal ones fall, because sun and wind matter too. That day-to-day instability is itself the finding — a fixed temperature ranking can't capture it.

What this does not claim. No "safe hours," no street-level precision (the underlying data is a ~25km grid), and no measured WBGT — it's estimated from weather variables. Full formulas, sources, and caveats on the methods page.

Glossary — the five terms this page uses
Dry-bulb temperature
The ordinary air temperature a regular thermometer reads — the number in weather reports and heat advisories.
Wet-bulb temperature
How hot it is once humidity is counted — a measure of how well sweating can still cool you. Always at or below the dry-bulb reading; the closer the two, the harder your body has to work.
Estimated WBGT
Wet Bulb Globe Temperature: the standard measure of heat stress for people working outdoors, combining heat, humidity, direct sun, and wind. "Estimated" because it's computed from weather data here, not read off a physical instrument.
Heat-stress limit (REL)
The WBGT above which continuous outdoor work gets risky, from NIOSH. It slides with how hard the work is — heavier work, lower limit — which is why there's a workload selector. Shown for acclimatized workers, the right setting for India's outdoor laborers.
Avoidance window & shoulder hours
Heat guidance says avoid the afternoon (we use the widest state version, 11am–5pm) and work the morning/evening "shoulders" instead. This dashboard flags shoulder hours that cross the heat-stress limit anyway — the hours the advice overlooks.
Misranking delta
How many places a city moves between the ordinary temperature ranking and the WBGT ranking today. ▲12 means it ranks 12 places more dangerous once humidity, sun, and wind are counted.
1991–2020 normal
What's typical for this exact calendar date in that city, averaged over 30 years — so "hot for July in Kolkata" means hotter than Kolkata's own usual July, not hotter than some all-India average.

Scientific definitions, formulas, and error bounds live on the methods page.

The overlooked hours

India's heat guidance tells outdoor workers to avoid the afternoon and shift work to the morning and evening. But on hot days those shoulder hours themselves cross the heat-stress limit — so the advice can send people into hours that aren't actually safe. Pick a workload and see how often that happens today, live.

“Outside the window” uses 11am–5pm — the widest afternoon-avoidance window any audited state Heat Action Plan uses (Odisha starts 11:00, Gujarat ends 17:00; IMD's national advice is 12–3). So these hours fall outside even the most generous version of the guidance — the count is a conservative lower bound. The limit shown is NIOSH's REL for acclimatized workers at the selected workload; see the methods page.
